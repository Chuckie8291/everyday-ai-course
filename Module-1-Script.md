## SLIDE 3: The Kitchen Analogy (0:25–3:30)

**[0:25]**
Before you type a single word, let me explain what's actually happening when you talk to an AI. Because once you understand this, everything else clicks.

And I'm going to explain it without a single technical term.

**[0:40]**
Here's the best way to think about AI: imagine a sous-chef who has read every cookbook ever written.

Every recipe. Every cooking technique. Every food blog. Every restaurant review. Every chemistry textbook about how heat affects proteins.

This sous-chef has never actually cooked in your kitchen. They've never tasted your food. They don't know if you prefer salt or not.

**[1:10]**
But when you say "I want to make something with chicken and rice," they can instantly pull from millions of recipes and give you options. They can adjust for dietary restrictions. They can create a shopping list. They can even suggest wine pairings.

**[1:30]**
That's what AI is. It has read an enormous amount of text — books, articles, websites, conversations — and it's really good at predicting what words should come next, given what you asked.

Is it actually intelligent? That's a philosophical question. What matters for you is this: it's incredibly useful.

**[2:00]**
Five things to remember:

One — it knows a lot. More than any human could read in a thousand lifetimes.

Two — it doesn't actually think or understand. It predicts words based on patterns.

Three — it can be wrong. Sometimes confidently wrong. You are always the boss.

**[2:30]**
Four — you guide it with your words. The quality of what you get back depends almost entirely on the quality of what you ask.

And five — better instructions equal better results. This is the single most important skill you'll learn in this course.

**[3:00]**
In a few minutes, you're going to experience this yourself. But first — let's look at what AI can and can't do, so you have realistic expectations.

## SLIDE 4: What AI Can and Can't Do (3:30–5:30)

**[3:30]**
Let's set expectations right.

Here's what AI is great at: answering questions, summarizing long documents, writing drafts, explaining complex topics in simple terms, and helping you brainstorm.

Need to understand a medical term? Ask the AI. Need a first draft of an email? The AI can write it. Have a long article you don't have time to read? Paste it in and ask for a summary. Stuck on a problem? Talk it through with the AI.

**[4:15]**
These are real, useful, everyday superpowers. They're available to you right now, for free.

Now — here's what AI cannot do. And this is just as important.

**[4:30]**
It doesn't know what happened after its training cutoff. If you ask about something that happened last week, it might not know.

It cannot access your personal information unless you share it. It doesn't know your email, your calendar, your bank account.

**[4:50]**
It's not always accurate. Sometimes — and this is important — AI makes things up. It sounds confident, but it's wrong. We call this hallucination, and we'll cover how to spot it later.

It cannot replace your judgment. If the decision matters — health, money, legal — use AI as input, not as the final word.

**[5:15]**
And it cannot read your mind. You have to tell it what you want. Which brings us to the most important skill in this course: asking good questions.

## SLIDE 13: Three Ingredients (13:40–16:30)

**[13:40]**
Now let's talk about the single most important skill you'll learn in this entire course: asking good questions.

This is not complicated. There's no secret code. It's just... being specific.

**[13:55]**
Here are the three ingredients of a good ask.

Ingredient one: be specific.

Bad question: "Tell me about Italy."

Good question: "What are the best towns to visit in Tuscany for a couple in their 50s who love food and wine?"

**[14:20]**
See the difference? The first one gets you a Wikipedia article. The second one gets you a personalized travel recommendation. The more detail you give, the more relevant the answer.

Ingredient two: give context.

Bad: "Write an email."

Good: "Write a polite email to my landlord explaining that the AC broke and I'd like it fixed this week."

**[14:55]**
Context is like giving directions. Without it, the AI has to guess what you want. With it, the response is exactly what you need.

Ingredient three: say what you want.

Bad: "Tell me about exercise."

Good: "Give me a 20-minute home workout for someone in their 50s, no equipment, with warm-up and cool-down included."

**[15:30]**
You asked for: a workout. You specified: 20 minutes, home, no equipment. You said who it's for: someone in their 50s. You asked for format: warm-up and cool-down included.

That's a great ask. And you'll get a great answer.

**[16:00]**
This isn't hard. It's the same skill you use when you're explaining what you want to another person. Be clear. Give details. Say what you need.

Let's look at a couple more examples.

## SLIDE 14: Before and After (16:30–18:00)

**[16:30]**
Let me show you the difference this makes with real examples.

Example one. Before: "Help me with dinner." That's vague. The AI has to guess.

After: "Give me 5 easy dinner ideas using chicken, rice, and broccoli. Under 30 minutes each. Include a shopping list."

**[17:00]**
That second prompt — specific, with context, saying exactly what you want — will get you an immediately useful answer. The first one might get you nowhere.

Example two. Before: "Write something for work." Again, too vague.

After: "Write a 3-paragraph project update email for my boss. We finished phase 1 on time, phase 2 starts next Monday, and we need approval for the budget increase."

**[17:40]**
That's specific. That's actionable. That's going to save you thirty minutes of staring at a blank screen.

This is the number one habit to build: before you hit Enter, ask yourself — have I given the AI enough to work with?

## SLIDE 16: Follow-Up Toolkit (18:10–21:00)

**[18:10]**
Okay. You've asked a good question. The AI responded. Now what?

Now you use your follow-up toolkit. These are five simple things you can say to make any answer better.

**[18:25]**
Tool number one: "Try again, but simpler."

If the AI gives you a complicated, jargon-filled answer, just say: "Explain that like I'm 12." Or "Give me the short version." The AI will rewrite its answer at whatever level you ask for.

**[18:45]**
Tool two: "Can you be more specific?"

Push for details. "What exactly do you mean by that?" or "Give me a specific example." Don't accept vague answers. Make the AI earn its keep.

**[19:05]**
Tool three: "What are my options?"

Get the AI to lay out alternatives. "What are three different approaches to this?" or "Give me the pros and cons of each option." This turns the AI from an answer machine into a thinking partner.

**[19:25]**
Tool four: "Format this as..."

Tell it how you want the information organized. "Put this in a table." "Write this as bullet points." "Make this a step-by-step list." You control the output format.

**[19:45]**
Tool five: "Check your work."

Ask the AI to verify itself. "Are you sure about that?" "Double-check that date." "Is there another source for this?" Sometimes it'll catch its own mistakes. Sometimes it won't — but asking never hurts.

**[20:10]**
These five techniques — simplify, specify, explore options, reformat, verify — they work in almost any conversation. Use them. They're how you go from getting answers to getting great answers.
