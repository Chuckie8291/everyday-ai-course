## SLIDE 3: Your New Writing Process (0:28–4:00)

**[0:28]**
Writing is probably the single biggest time sink in most professional jobs. Reports. Proposals. Emails. Newsletters. Performance reviews. Client updates.

And most of that time isn't spent writing. It's spent staring at a blank screen, trying to find the first sentence.

**[0:50]**
Here's your new process. Four steps.

Step one: give the AI a brain dump. Don't write. Just talk. "I need to write a quarterly report. Here's what happened: we launched two features, revenue grew twelve percent, we lost our biggest client. Turn this into a two-page executive summary."

**[1:20]**
You just threw a bunch of messy thoughts at the AI, and in about fifteen seconds, you get back a structured, coherent draft. It's not perfect. But it's no longer a blank screen.

Step two: review and guide. Read the draft. Tell the AI what to fix. "Make the tone more optimistic. Add a section about team achievements. Shorten the client loss paragraph — focus on what we learned, not what went wrong."

**[1:55]**
Step three: polish your voice. This is the one most people miss. Tell the AI how you sound. "Rewrite this in my voice. I'm direct and informal. I use short sentences. I never say leverage or synergy."

Step four: final pass. "Proofread this for typos and grammar. Then give me three subject line options for the email."

**[2:30]**
That's it. Brain dump, review, voice polish, final pass. What used to take an hour now takes ten minutes. And the quality is better — because you spent your energy on thinking and editing, not on wrestling with the first draft.

## SLIDE 4: Everything You Write — Upgraded (4:00–6:00)

**[4:00]**
Let's look at specific scenarios.

Reports and proposals. "Draft a three-page project proposal. Include: problem statement, proposed solution, timeline, budget estimate, and risks." The AI structures it. You add the specifics.

**[4:25]**
Client emails. "A client is unhappy about a delay. Write an empathetic response that acknowledges their frustration, explains what happened, and outlines our fix timeline." The AI writes something you'd send — not defensive, not groveling. Professional and human.

**[4:50]**
Newsletters. "Write a monthly newsletter for our customers. This month: new feature launched, we hired three people, and we're hosting a webinar. Keep it warm and brief."

Job descriptions and performance reviews. "Write a performance review for a marketing manager who exceeded targets and mentored two juniors, but struggles with meeting deadlines. Be constructive."

**[5:30]**
Notice the pattern every time: you provide the facts and the context. The AI provides the structure and the first draft. You provide the judgment and the final polish.

This is not the AI replacing you as a writer. This is you becoming a much faster, more effective writer.

## SLIDE 5: 3.2 Title — Meetings (6:00–6:10)

---

## SLIDE 6: Meetings Scenarios (6:10–9:00)

**[6:10]**
Meetings. For many of us, they're where information goes to die.

AI changes that — before, during, and after.

Before the meeting: create the agenda. "I'm running a 30-minute team sync. We need to cover project X status, blockers, hiring update, and Q4 planning. Create a timed agenda." You walk in with a clear plan.

**[6:45]**
After the meeting: this is where AI really shines. If you have a transcript or even rough notes, paste them in. "Summarize this meeting. What were the three key decisions? Who's responsible for each action item? List them by owner and deadline."

**[7:10]**
In thirty seconds, you have a clean summary and an action item list. No more "wait, who was supposed to do what?"

Extract action items: "From these notes, pull out every action item. Format as task — owner — deadline. Flag anything without a clear owner." Now nothing falls through the cracks.

**[7:40]**
Follow-up email: "Write a follow-up email to all attendees summarizing the meeting. Include decisions made, next steps with owners, and the date of our next meeting."

Five minutes after the meeting ends, everyone has exactly what they need. Including you.

**[8:20]**
One more thing: if you're the kind of person who takes sparse notes because you're too busy participating — that's fine. The AI can work with rough notes. Bullet points. Half-sentences. It's good at filling in the blanks. Just give it what you've got.

## SLIDE 7: 3.3 Title — Research (9:00–9:10)

---

## SLIDE 8: Research Scenarios (9:10–12:00)

**[9:10]**
Research is one of the areas where AI creates the most leverage. What used to take hours of Googling, reading, and synthesizing now takes minutes.

Market and competitor research. "Summarize the top five competitors to our company. For each: their main product, pricing, target customer, and one thing they do better than us."

**[9:40]**
You get a structured competitive analysis. You can drill into any competitor: "Go deeper on competitor three. What's their funding situation? What do their customer reviews say?"

Document summaries. "I have a fifty-page industry report. Summarize the five most important findings. Then tell me: what should our company do differently based on this?"

**[10:15]**
This is a superpower. You don't need to read every report. The AI reads it for you and surfaces what matters.

Data interpretation. "Here's our Q2 sales data. What trends stand out? What's one thing we should investigate further?"

**[10:45]**
Fact-checking. "Verify these three claims from a vendor's proposal. Are they accurate? Are the sources credible? Flag anything that seems exaggerated."

This is especially important. Use AI to check other people's claims. It's a great BS detector.

**[11:20]**
A word of caution, and I want to be very clear about this: always verify AI research against original sources. AI can hallucinate — it can confidently state facts, statistics, and citations that aren't real. Especially for niche or specialized topics.

Think of AI research as a starting point, not the final answer. Use it to get oriented fast. Then verify what matters.

## SLIDE 9: 3.4 Title — Spreadsheets (12:00–12:10)

---

## SLIDE 10: Spreadsheet Demo (12:10–14:30)

**[12:10]**
I want to show you something that might change your relationship with spreadsheets forever.

You don't need to know formulas. You don't need to know pivot tables. You just need to describe what you want in plain English.

**[12:25]**
Here's an example. Say to the AI: "I have a spreadsheet with columns for date, customer, product, revenue, and region. I need to find which region had the highest growth month-over-month, and which product is declining. Walk me through it step by step."

**[12:50]**
The AI doesn't just give you the answer. It walks you through the process. "First, sort your data by region and date. Then for each region, calculate the growth rate using this formula — paste this into cell F2 and drag it down. For the declining product, create a pivot table. Here's how..."

**[13:20]**
And here's the best part: if you get stuck, you can say "I'm stuck on step three — what do you mean by drag it down?" or "Can you explain pivot tables like I've never heard of them?" The AI adjusts. It explains again, differently. It never gets frustrated.

**[13:50]**
This is not just for complex analysis. You can ask: "How do I freeze the top row in Excel?" "How do I remove duplicates?" "What's the formula for calculating percentage change?"

Every spreadsheet question you've ever Googled — the AI can answer, with step-by-step instructions specific to your version and your data.

## SLIDE 11: More Spreadsheet Scenarios (14:30–16:00)

**[14:30]**
A few more things you can do.

Create charts: "My data shows monthly sales for 2025. Tell me which chart type would best show the trend, and give me the exact steps to create it."

Clean messy data: "I have a list of 500 names in Last, First format and I need First Last. Also, some have middle initials. How do I fix this?"

**[15:00]**
Budget analysis: "Here's our department budget versus actuals. Calculate the variance for each line item. Highlight anything over ten percent off. Suggest three areas to cut."

Connect the dots: "I have sales data in one sheet and customer satisfaction scores in another. How do I combine these to see if happy customers spend more?"

**[15:35]**
You are now dangerous in Excel — without memorizing a single formula. That's the promise of this module: AI handles the mechanics. You focus on the thinking.

## SLIDE 12: 3.5 Title — Presentations (16:00–16:10)

---

## SLIDE 13: Presentation Workflow (16:10–18:00)

**[16:10]**
Building a presentation used to mean staring at a blank slide deck, trying to figure out the structure before you'd even figured out the story.

Not anymore.

**[16:22]**
Say to the AI: "I need to present our Q3 results to the leadership team. Key points: revenue up 18%, we launched product X, customer churn dropped from 4% to 2.5%, and we're requesting budget for 3 new hires. Create a slide-by-slide outline for a 15-minute presentation."

**[16:50]**
What you get back is a complete narrative arc. Slide one: title. Slide two: executive summary with three bullets. Slide three: revenue deep-dive with chart ideas. Slide four: product launch story. Slide five: customer retention before-and-after. Slide six: team expansion request with justification. Slide seven: Q4 priorities and close.

**[17:20]**
Then you say: "Now write the speaker notes for each slide. Keep it conversational — I don't read from scripts."

And now you have the slide structure and the words to say. Not just bullet points — actual talking points, in your voice, with transitions between slides.

**[17:45]**
You go from "I have a presentation next week and I haven't started" to "I have a complete outline and speaker notes and I just need to design the slides" — in about ten minutes.

---

## SLIDE 14: Presentation Pro Tips (18:00–19:30)

**[18:00]**
A few more presentation superpowers.

Find your story: "Here are ten facts about our project. Help me find the narrative — what's the most compelling story these numbers tell?"

Data to visuals: "I have these metrics. For each one, suggest the best chart type and what the chart should emphasize. I want the audience to get it in five seconds."

**[18:30]**
Anticipate questions: "Based on this presentation outline, what are the five toughest questions the leadership team might ask? Help me prepare answers for each."

Time it right: "I have ten minutes to present. Here's my outline. Tell me how many minutes to spend on each slide. Flag anything I should cut to stay on time."

**[19:00]**
You are now the most prepared person in the room. Every single time.
