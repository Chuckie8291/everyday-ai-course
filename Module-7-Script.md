## SLIDE 3: Keep This to Yourself (0:28–4:00)

**[0:28]**
Before we talk about the future, we need to talk about safety. Not in a scary way — in a smart, practical way.

When you type something into an AI, that information goes to the company's servers. It may be used to improve their models. It may be stored in logs. It may be reviewed by human employees for quality control.

**[0:55]**
Most of the time, that's fine. "How do I make scrambled eggs?" — nobody cares. "Summarize this public article" — completely fine.

But some things should never, ever go into an AI.

**[1:10]**
Personal identifying information. Your Social Security number. Your driver's license. Your passport number. Your full name combined with your birth date. Identity theft starts with the basics. Keep them out.

Financial details. Bank account numbers. Credit card numbers. Full tax returns with your name and SSN. You can ask AI to analyze spending categories or explain tax concepts. Just strip out the identifiers first.

**[1:50]**
Company secrets. Unreleased products. Internal financials. Strategy documents. Client lists. If it would hurt your company if it leaked, don't paste it into a public AI tool.

Medical records. Full records with your name attached. It's fine to ask "what does this blood test result mean" — just remove your name, your doctor's name, and any identifiers first.

**[2:25]**
Passwords and API keys. Never. Under any circumstances. If you paste a password into an AI, it's stored in a log somewhere. If that log is ever compromised, so is your password.

Other people's information. Don't share your kids' details, your parents' medical info, your clients' data, your employees' personal information. Not without their permission.

**[3:00]**
This might sound like a long list of don'ts. But the rule is actually very simple. I call it the Billboard Test.

Before you paste anything into an AI, ask yourself: "Would I be comfortable seeing this on a billboard in my hometown?"

If the answer is no — don't share it.

**[3:30]**
You can still get incredible value from AI without sharing sensitive information. Be smart. Protect yourself and the people you're responsible for.

## SLIDE 5: 7.2 Title — Spotting Mistakes (4:45–4:55)

---

## SLIDE 6: When to Be Skeptical (4:55–8:00)

**[4:55]**
AI sounds confident. That's its default setting. Even when it's completely wrong, it sounds like it knows exactly what it's talking about.

You need to know when to be skeptical.

**[5:10]**
Dates and numbers. AI loves inventing specific dates. "The Treaty of Whatever was signed in 1847." Maybe it was. Maybe it was 1848. Maybe the treaty doesn't exist. Question every specific date, statistic, and dollar amount. Ask: "Are you sure about that date? Cite a source."

**[5:35]**
Citations and quotes. "As Shakespeare wrote in Hamlet..." AI is notorious for inventing quotes and attributing them to famous people. If there's no verifiable source, assume the quote was made up. AI wants to sound authoritative. Inventing a Shakespeare quote makes it sound authoritative.

**[6:00]**
Biographical details. "Dr. Smith published this groundbreaking paper in 2019..." AI invents people. It invents papers. It invents entire academic careers. If the information matters, verify that the person actually exists and actually said what the AI claims.

**[6:25]**
Niche and specialized topics. AI is great at broad, common knowledge. The more specialized the topic, the more likely it hallucinates. For obscure historical events, technical specifications, or specialized professional knowledge — treat everything as suspect until verified.

**[6:50]**
Too-perfect answers. If the AI gives you exactly what you wanted to hear — perfectly matching your assumptions — be extra skeptical. AI is very good at telling you what you want to hear. That's not the same as telling you what's true.

**[7:15]**
Fake URLs and references. AI sometimes generates plausible-looking but entirely fake web addresses, academic references, and citations. Always click the link before relying on it.

The bottom line: trust, but verify. AI is a brilliant assistant. It's also a convincing liar. Your judgment is the final filter.

## SLIDE 7: 7.3 Title — AI Scams (8:00–8:10)

---

## SLIDE 8: The New Scams (8:10–10:30)

**[8:10]**
Scammers love new technology. Every time something new goes mainstream, they find ways to exploit it. AI is no different.

AI phishing emails. Scammers used to be easy to spot — bad grammar, weird formatting, obviously fake. Now they use AI to write perfect emails that look exactly like your bank, your boss, or your family. The old giveaways are gone.

**[8:40]**
Your defense hasn't changed: verify through a different channel. If your "bank" emails you about a problem, don't click the link. Call the number on the back of your card. If your "boss" emails asking for an urgent wire transfer, call them. The extra thirty seconds saves you from a very bad day.

**[9:05]**
Fake AI tools. "Download this free AI assistant — it does everything ChatGPT does but better!" It's malware. Only download AI apps from official app stores or well-known websites. If a tool promises amazing AI features for free, ask yourself how they're making money. They're probably making it by stealing your data.

**[9:35]**
Voice cloning. This one is scary. AI can clone someone's voice from three seconds of audio. Scammers call: "Grandma, I'm in trouble. I need money. Please don't tell Mom." It sounds exactly like your grandchild.

Establish a family code word. Something only your family knows. If you get a panicked call asking for money — even if the voice sounds right — ask for the code word. No code word, no money.

**[10:10]**
Fake news and deepfakes. AI can generate realistic images, videos, and news articles in seconds. If something seems too outrageous, pause. Check other sources. Don't be the person who shares a fake photo because it confirmed what you already wanted to believe.

## SLIDE 9: 7.4 Title — Ethics (10:30–10:40)

---

## SLIDE 10: Four Ethical Guidelines (10:40–13:00)

**[10:40]**
With everything you can now do comes a responsibility to do it well.

Four guidelines.

One: be transparent. When you use AI to create something significant — a report, an article, a piece of art — say so. "Written with AI assistance." "Image generated with AI." Transparency builds trust. Secrecy destroys it. People don't mind that you used AI. They mind when you pretend you didn't.

**[11:20]**
Two: don't plagiarize. AI output is a starting point. Add your thinking. Rewrite in your voice. Verify the facts. You are the author. The AI was a tool — like spell-check, like a calculator. You wouldn't turn in a math assignment and say "my calculator did this." Same principle.

**[11:50]**
Three: AI augments, not replaces. Your judgment matters. AI suggests. You decide. This is especially important for decisions about people — hiring, promotions, evaluations. AI can inform those decisions. A human must make the final call. Always.

**[12:20]**
Four: teach the next generation. If you have kids or grandkids, show them how to use AI as a learning tool, not a cheating tool. Teach them to ask AI to explain a concept — not just give the answer. "Help me understand why" instead of "tell me the answer." You're modeling what responsible AI use looks like. They're watching.

## SLIDE 11: 7.5 Title — Your Journey (13:00–13:10)

## SLIDE 12: Staying Current (13:10–16:00)

**[13:10]**
You've completed the course. Now what? How do you keep growing without getting overwhelmed by the firehose of AI news?

Five things.

**[13:25]**
One: pick one newsletter. Not five. One. TLDR AI is good. The Rundown. Superhuman. Pick one that takes five minutes to read. Unsubscribe from everything else. You'll know what matters without drowning in headlines.

**[13:45]**
Two: practice weekly. Use AI for one real thing every day — even if it's just meal planning or drafting a quick email. The habit matters more than the complexity. You don't stay fit by reading about exercise. You stay fit by exercising.

**[14:05]**
Three: join the community. Learning alone is hard. Join our community — Discord, Facebook group, whatever works. Share prompts. Ask questions. Help others who are where you were two modules ago. Teaching is the fastest way to cement your own learning.

**[14:30]**
Four: monthly check-in. Once a month, look at what you're doing manually. Ask: could an agent handle this? Build one. Your automation roadmap grows one agent at a time. In six months, you'll have a small team of digital assistants doing work you used to do yourself.

**[15:00]**
Five: share what you know. You are now more AI-literate than roughly ninety percent of the population. Seriously. Most people still think AI is either magic or a threat. You know it's a tool — powerful, imperfect, and incredibly useful when used well.

Share what you've learned. With your friends. Your family. Your coworkers. You don't need to be an expert to help someone take their first step.
