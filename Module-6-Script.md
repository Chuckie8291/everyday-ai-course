## SLIDE 2: 6.1 Title — What's an Agent? (0:20–0:30)

---

## SLIDE 3: Chatbot vs Agent (0:30–3:30)

**[0:30]**
Everything we've done so far in this course has been with chatbots. You type a question. The AI responds. You type another question. It responds again.

That's powerful. But it has a limit: you have to be there. You have to ask. Every single time.

**[0:50]**
An AI agent is different.

A chatbot answers your questions. An agent does your tasks.

**[1:00]**
Let me make this concrete. You say to a chatbot: "What's the weather?" It tells you. You say to an agent: "Schedule a meeting with the team next Tuesday." The agent checks everyone's calendar. Finds an open slot. Sends the invites. Adds an agenda. Confirms with you when it's done.

**[1:30]**
You didn't tell it HOW to do any of that. You didn't say "first check the calendar, then find a slot, then send the invite." You just gave it the goal. It figured out the steps. It used tools. It took actions.

This is the leap. From asking to delegating.

**[2:00]**
A chatbot is reactive — it waits for you. An agent is proactive — it works toward a goal. A chatbot uses one tool: language. An agent uses many tools: calendars, email, web search, spreadsheets, databases.

A chatbot stops when you stop asking. An agent keeps working until the task is done — even if that takes multiple steps, even if it hits a problem and has to try a different approach.

**[3:00]**
You are no longer the operator. You're the manager. You set the direction. The agent handles the execution.

---

## SLIDE 4: The Personal Assistant Analogy (3:30–5:30)

**[3:30]**
Think of it like this. A chatbot is like asking a librarian a question. They tell you the answer. "The book you want is on the third floor, aisle seven." Helpful. But then they wait for your next question.

An AI agent is like having a personal assistant. You say: "I need to set up meetings with these five people next week."

**[4:00]**
The librarian tells you how to set up a meeting. "Here's how you use the calendar app."

The assistant just does it. Checks availability. Finds slots. Sends invites. Handles the conflicts. Comes back and says: "Done. Three are confirmed, two had conflicts so I proposed alternatives. Here's the summary."

**[4:35]**
The assistant has permission to use tools on your behalf. They can think through multi-step problems. They can make decisions within boundaries you set. They can recover from errors without bothering you.

You're not typing questions anymore. You're delegating work.

**[5:00]**
And here's what makes this real: these agents exist today. Right now. Some are built into the tools you already use. Others you can build yourself — in minutes — with no code. That's what this module is about.

## SLIDE 6: Agents Available Now (5:40–8:30)

**[5:40]**
You don't have to build from scratch. There are agents built into the tools you're already using.

ChatGPT's browsing capability. When you ask it to research something, it doesn't just give you an answer it memorized. It searches the web. It reads multiple pages. It synthesizes what it finds. It decided what to search, what to read, what to combine. That's agent behavior.

**[6:15]**
ChatGPT's data analysis. Upload a spreadsheet. Say: "Analyze this data and create charts showing the trends." The agent writes code. Runs it. Creates visualizations. Explains what it found. You never touched a formula. You never opened Excel. The agent did the work.

**[6:40]**
Claude's computer use. This one's remarkable. Claude can actually control a computer — clicking, typing, navigating websites. You say: "Go to this website, find the pricing page, and summarize the three plan options." Claude does it, step by step, like a person at the keyboard.

**[7:10]**
Perplexity's research mode. It searches multiple sources, compares information, synthesizes findings, and cites everything. It doesn't just answer — it investigates.

Scheduling agents. Tools like Motion, Reclaim, and Clockwise. You tell them your priorities. They schedule your time. They adapt when your plans change. You set the strategy. They handle the logistics.

**[7:50]**
Every one of these is available today. Most have free tiers. None require coding. You can start using agents — real agents — right now.

## SLIDE 7: 6.3 Title — Build Your First (8:30–8:40)

---

## SLIDE 8: Step by Step (8:40–11:30)

**[8:40]**
Now let's build your own. Fifteen minutes. No code. I'll walk you through it.

Step one. Pick your platform. I recommend Make.com — it's more visual and the free tier is generous. Zapier is also excellent. Both work the same way: drag, drop, connect.

**[9:05]**
Step two. Choose a trigger — the thing that wakes your agent up. "Every morning at 8 AM." "When I add a task to my to-do list." "When I get an email from a specific person." The trigger is the "when."

Step three. Connect to AI. Add an AI step to your automation. Paste your API key. Write instructions. For example: "Read this task. Break it into three to five subtasks. For each, estimate how long it will take. Format the output as a checklist."

**[9:50]**
Step four. Define the output. Where should the results go? Back to your to-do list as subtasks? Emailed to you? Added to a document? The agent needs to know what to do with its work.

Step five. Test and activate. Run it once manually. Did it work? Did the output look right? If not, tweak the instructions and try again. When it works, flip the switch. Your agent is now live. It will run automatically — every morning, every new task, every matching email — without you touching anything.

**[10:45]**
Here's the most important advice I can give you: start simple. One trigger. One action. Get that working perfectly before you add complexity. The instinct is to build a masterpiece. Build a paper airplane first. Make sure it flies.

## SLIDE 9: 6.4 Title — Real Workflows (11:30–11:40)

---

## SLIDE 10: Agents in the Wild (11:40–14:30)

**[11:40]**
Let me show you what real agents look like in practice. These aren't hypotheticals. People are using these today.

The email triage agent. Wakes up every hour. Checks your inbox. Labels every email: urgent, needs reply, newsletter, spam. For the ones that need a reply, it drafts a response. You open your drafts folder, review, and send. Fifteen minutes instead of an hour.

**[12:15]**
The research agent. You say: "Research this topic and send me a daily briefing." Every morning, it searches the web, reads the top articles, and emails you a three-paragraph summary with links. You show up to work already informed. You didn't spend an hour reading.

**[12:40]**
The social media agent. You provide a topic. It generates a week of posts. Suggests images. Schedules them. Monitors engagement. You review once a week instead of posting every day.

**[13:00]**
The client follow-up agent. After every meeting, it sends a personalized follow-up summarizing what was discussed. After every proposal, it checks in after five days if there's no response. Nothing falls through the cracks. Not because you're more organized — because your agent is.

**[13:30]**
The weekly report agent. Every Friday at 4 PM, it pulls data from your project management tool, your calendar, your time tracker. Generates a "what got done this week" report. Emails it to whoever needs it. You leave the office on time.

**[13:55]**
Notice the pattern. Every one of these is: trigger, AI does something useful, output goes somewhere you can review. You're the editor. The agent is the first draft. And the first draft happens whether you're there or not.

## SLIDE 11: 6.5 Title — Automating Tasks (14:30–14:40)

---

## SLIDE 12: What to Automate First (14:40–17:00)

**[14:40]**
Here's how to figure out what to automate. I want you to do a personal automation audit. For one week, notice every task you do that meets these criteria.

It's repetitive — you do it every day, every week, or every month. It follows the same pattern each time. It doesn't require your unique creativity or judgment. And you kind of hate doing it.

**[15:15]**
Common candidates. Sorting and replying to routine emails. Creating weekly status reports. Scheduling meetings across multiple people. Transcribing and summarizing meeting notes. Researching topics for projects or clients. Posting to social media on a schedule. Following up on unanswered emails or proposals.

**[15:50]**
Pick one. Just one. Automate it this week. Live with it for a week. Notice what works and what doesn't. Tweak it. Then pick the next one.

The biggest mistake people make is trying to automate everything at once. They build five agents, none of them work quite right, and they give up. Don't do that. One agent. Make it reliable. Then expand.

---

## SLIDE 13: Your Automation Roadmap (17:00–18:30)

**[17:00]**
Here's a realistic roadmap.

Weeks one and two: start simple. Automate one thing. Email sorting. Task breakdown. A daily morning briefing. Get comfortable with the pattern — trigger, AI, output.

**[17:20]**
Weeks three and four: add a second agent. Pick something that genuinely annoys you every week. Build an agent that handles it. Now you have two things running in the background, saving you time every day.

**[17:40]**
Month two: connect agents together. Agent one summarizes your day. Agent two turns that summary into tomorrow's to-do list. Agent three prioritizes the list by importance. They work together — a small team of digital assistants.

**[18:05]**
Month three and beyond: maintenance and expansion. Review what's working. Tweak instructions for agents that aren't quite right. Retire ones that aren't useful. Build new ones as you spot opportunities. The goal isn't to automate everything. It's to free up your energy for the work that actually needs you.
